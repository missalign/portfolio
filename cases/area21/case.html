Área 21 – Creativity Maker Lab

Designed an innovative public service learning journey and flexible maker space that empowers youth through project-based learning, creative problem-solving, and hands-on experimentation with emerging technologies.

Context

Developed when Tellus (Public Service Design Agency) in partnership with the State Government of São Paulo, Instituto Ana Rosa, and key tech sponsors, Área 21 is a network of educational laboratories. The service provides adolescents aged 14 to 17 with structured, project-based journeys designed to build critical 21st-century skills, expand technical repertories, and democratize access to new technologies.


Challenge

Educational System

  • Traditional, rigid educational public formats fail to engage youth
  • Complex, intimidating technical tools (Arduino, 3D printing) require accessible onboarding
  • Scaling a framework across different local contexts and nuances
  • Coordinating multiple institutional stakeholders and co-educators

    • Students

  • Lack of connection to standard technical curricula
  • Fear of making mistakes or failing in public settings
  • Need for individual autonomy and active personal agency
  • Seeking spaces to collaborate safely on self-directed passions

    • Discovery & Research

    Core Service Principles Matrix:

  • Foster Youth Agency: Build systems that center student autonomy.
  • Empathy-Led Journeys: Respect and adapt to local cultural nuances.
  • The Maker Mindset: Reframe mistakes as vital learning iterations.
  • Complex Problem Solving: Bridge high-tech skills with real-world applications.

    • Design Exploration & Delivery

    The solution was designed as a dual-phase service blueprint spanning a 12-week lifecycle, supported by custom physical spaces, specialized educational toolkits, and dual-teacher facilitator frameworks.

    Impact

    Direct Service Scope

  • 240 Youth Impacted: Successfully executed across two distinct intensive semester cycles at Instituto Ana Rosa by late 2017.
  • 100% Unique Journeys: Every student experienced personalized skill rotations via customizable project missions.

    • Social Innovation

  • Rizoma Demo Day: Dozens of youth-led solutions built and pitched to elite panels of specialists, makers, and entrepreneurs.
  • Indirect Social Impact: Expanded tech advocacy, family community integration, and local visibility for public socio-educational design.

    • Infrastructure Assets

  • Flexible Maker Spaces: Fully operational laboratory zones built around 3D printers, laser cutters, vinyl plotters, and VR tech.
  • Custom Didactic Materials: Proprietary learning blocks, mission logs, and technical user guides authored explicitly for the youth demographic.

    • Reflection

  • Scaffolded Autonomy Works: Dividing the service into a rigid tech onboarding phase (Cycles 1–4) followed by an open, self-managed free challenge (Rizoma) successfully minimized technical intimidation.
  • Co-Design with Ecosystems: Aligning public sector goals with private corporate funding (Samsung, Vivo, Telefônica) requires highly detailed physical touchpoints and localized visual storytelling.
  • Elevating Presentation as a Metric: Including a structured pitch to outside experts transforms a technical lab project into a deep, emotional celebration of community capability.
